01

The Setup

An acquisition. A re-platform. A once-in-a-decade chance.

In 2021, Salesforce acquired Evergage to enter the personalization market. By 2023, leadership was re-platforming the product onto Salesforce's new Customer Data Platform — now Data Cloud / Data 360 — to build a native, on-core personalization engine every cloud could plug into.

Off-core customers were losing confidence. On-core anticipation had created a "wait and see" freeze. ACV, AOV, and pipeline velocity were stalling. Redesign and consolidation became a business priority — not a roadmap item.

"Get this right and Personalization becomes the AI/data layer every cloud plugs into. Get it wrong and the acquisition was a write-off."
02

The Design Problem

Engineering-led. Politically tangled. No seat for design.

Customers couldn't deliver personalized, timely messages at scale across Marketing, Sales, and Service. The org meant to fix that wasn't set up to.

  • No on-core engine existed. Salesforce had no native personalization layer built directly on Data Cloud.
  • Engineering-driven decisions. Architecture and engineering led; UX was downstream and rarely consulted.
  • Politically misaligned. Competing exec opinions, ambiguous team and product structures.
  • Beginner platform fluency. Even the engineering org was learning the Salesforce core platform we were building on top of.

The real question wasn't "what should the UI look like?" It was: how do you turn a fragmented, technical product into something a marketer can adopt in minutes — without breaking the on-platform conventions every Salesforce admin already trusts?

The before state — three problems, one product.

The Reframe

I authored the picture leadership and engineering both started pointing at.

Before any pixels, the org needed a shared mental model. So my first move wasn't a screen — it was a diagram.

The runtime engine matters, but it's invisible. The marketer's setup experience IS the platform — that's where trust, adoption, and ACV are won or lost. So I drafted a systems diagram that shifted the whole org's mental model: from "data-model-first feature" to "channel-first decisioning layer."

The shared mental model. The reusable abstraction in the middle — initially called "Personalization Point" internally — became the contract between channels and decisioning that every cloud now builds against. Customers told us they thought of it as a Personalization Campaign, and that's what shipped.
05

Research

Audit, benchmark, three rounds of testing.

The diagram opened the door. Research is what proved the pattern — and exposed which conventions had to break.

Not "four neat rounds." A real arc with leadership pressure, user pushback, and a vindicating partnership. Here's how it actually unfolded.

01
Joined, audited, found the issues
First weeks on the team: audited the existing app and platform. Documented the three problems shown in section 02 — disjointed object tabs, modal-heavy flows with technical jargon, properties dialogs requiring a 121-page help doc.
02
Dug into existing research → established benchmark need
Reviewed all prior research artifacts. Realized no one had benchmarked the current product against a clean baseline. Made the case for funded benchmark research before another design cycle.
03
Benchmark research with UX researcher
Partnered with a UX researcher to run the benchmark. Findings were severe: onboarding friction, terminology confusion, no guidance, time-on-task far too long. Established the baseline pain on paper.
04
Round 1 — modern mental-model approach
Tested a user-mental-model-led approach: channel-first, guidance embedded in the wizard rail, fused audience+content step. Users loved it. Time-on-task dropped, terminology confusion disappeared.
05
Round 2 — Salesforce-only pattern (leadership pressure)
Leadership pushed back: "It needs to look like the rest of Salesforce." Designed and tested a strict Salesforce-component-only version with the platform's data-first IA. Users did not like it. The same friction returned. Research, not opinion, settled it.
06
Cross-cloud partnership — co-authoring a new guided create pattern
Discovered Sales Cloud UX was hitting the same wall on their own configuration flows. Partnered with their design leader to co-author a guided create pattern that satisfied both the platform constraint and the user mental model. The signal in my product became a problem statement for the company.
07
Round 3 — the new pattern, validated
Tested the co-authored pattern. Users loved it. Same task-completion gains as Round 1, this time with leadership endorsement and a cross-cloud foundation under it.
08
Integrated into Marketing Cloud Campaigns
Took the validated pattern into Marketing Cloud — Personalization Campaigns now live inside the email builder. The pattern shipped where marketers already work.
"Three rounds of research. The research, not the loudest voice, settled the pattern. That's now an established Salesforce design pattern used across multiple clouds."

Workshop & Problem Solving

Aligning SVP-level leaders, engineering, and cross-cloud design — before a single Figma frame.

Research told us what was broken. The workshop is where I aligned the people who could greenlight or kill the work — and turned ambiguity into a shared, ratified plan.

I drafted concept mocks and pitched them to the SVP of Product. She backed them on the spot and flew me to San Francisco to lead a three-day executive alignment workshop — one exec, one PM, two engineers, and the design org in the same room. The Designs

The wizard, end-to-end

Scroll to walk Frank through it — Data → Strategy → Channel → Template → Decisions → Review. Each step asks one question, in marketer's vocabulary, with a single primary action.

The wizard isn't a UI pattern — it's the workshop's mental model in software. Where → What → Who → How, made enforceable.

From Salesforce-standard chrome to a marketer's mental model.

The workshop gave us a direction. The designs are where four key decisions got made — each one a tradeoff between Salesforce convention and the marketer's mental model.

The old pattern looked tidy and on-brand. Customers couldn't use it. The new pattern broke convention where convention got in the way — and won.

A
Naming as positioning — "Decisions," not "Variations"

Marketers told us "variations" felt futile — it underrepresented what the engine actually does. I pushed for "Decisions" across UI, docs, and sales decks because it reframes the product as a decisioning engine, not a content-swap tool. Vocabulary is product.

Tradeoff: Required cross-functional alignment with content design and PMM, but anchored a sharper product narrative for sales and customers.
B
Guidance as a layer, not a tab

The old pattern siloed guidance in its own tab. Research showed users abandoned it after the first visit. I embedded progress and next-best-action directly into the wizard rail itself, so guidance is always present without a context switch.

Tradeoff: More information density per step — but eliminated the empty-tab problem and dramatically improved task completion in usability testing.
C
Channel-first IA, even when the platform is data-first

Salesforce convention starts with the data model. Marketers don't think that way — 85%+ of research participants said "which channel" was their first question. I made channel the spine and data the substrate. Convention got broken because the user said so.

Tradeoff: Departed from Salesforce platform convention — required executive workshop alignment to ratify, but became the foundation of the cross-cloud pattern.
D
Audience + Content fused into one Decision step

Salesforce convention separates audience targeting from content configuration into discrete objects. Marketers think "this audience sees this content" as a single thought. I fused them into one Decision step with immediate visualization of who-sees-what — supporting multiple audiences per personalization.

Tradeoff: Required new data-model affordances from engineering, but matched the marketer's mental model exactly and removed a major drop-off point.

10

What I'd Do Differently

I'd push for content design and PMM at the table from week one. The "Decisions vs Variations" rename was the right call, but it came late — after research had already exposed how much language was costing us. Naming is positioning, and positioning is a cross-functional decision. I'd run that conversation up front next time.

I'd codify the systems diagram as a public artifact earlier. Once leadership and engineering started pointing at it, alignment moved 10× faster. I should have authored that picture in week two, not month four. Diagrams are leverage.

I'd build the cross-cloud coalition sooner. The signal from other cloud designers turned out to be the unlock for executive buy-in. If I'd started those conversations as relationship-building from day one — instead of finding the parallel pain in research round four — the pattern would have shipped a quarter earlier.

These aren't failures — they're the natural result of working alone inside a politically tangled, engineering-led org. But they shaped how I approach platform UX today: alignment artifacts, language, and coalitions are not afterthoughts.
